Finally the UI Design Begins

HCI Blog Series (5/9)

Learning the Art of UI Design

Until now, I’d been thinking mostly in terms of ideas and research. The moment I opened Figma and saw the blank canvas

Exciting? Sure. Intimidating? Very. UI design is where you stop talking about helping users—and actually start showing them something that helps.

Of all the design principles, hierarchy and accessibility made the biggest impression. I’ve seen enough cluttered interfaces to know what not to do, but understanding *why* something feels clean or confusing—that was new. I found myself questioning my old habits, like centering everything (it turns out left-aligned info is often more usable!) and relying on color alone to guide users.

Designing the Medium-Fidelity Prototype

I used Figma for my prototype. At first, I was just dropping elements in and arranging them like a mood board. But then I started applying actual feedback and structure—turning those pieces into coherent screens.

Our early wireframes were a mess—boxes everywhere, no visual rhythm. But as I applied our earlier user feedback (like the need for quick access to scholarship filters), the layout began to breathe and become fairly minimalist but still workable for mobile and desktop.

Challenges & Breakthroughs

Biggest challenge? Navigation. I kept adding links, thinking more access meant better usability. But then I got lost in my own menu.

I took a step back and asked, “What would I want to see first if I knew nothing?” That’s when I re-centered the homepage to feature three primary flows: Search, Scholarships, and Compare Schools. Everything else moved into a secondary menu.

One of the most surprising moments came when I swapped the “school card” layout based on someone’s casual remark: “It looks too academic, like I’m reading a form.” I turned that card into a more inviting panel with icons, highlights, and a short blurb—and suddenly it clicked.

Personal Reflection: Designing for the User

This process taught me that UI design isn’t just about looking nice it’s about feeling right for someone who needs it. Every button and layout choice asks the same question: “Will this make someone’s life easier?”

I used to think UI was just decoration on top of function. Now I see it as the bridge. It is the interaction. It’s where the idea meets the human.

If I could go back and tell myself one thing, it would be: Sketch messy. Test fast. And never get too attached with your first version. Iteration is good, but frankly no one was complaining with what we got going from medium to high fi.
